# Terence Tao - Mathematical Philosophy & Problem-Solving

## Three Phases of Mathematical Education (from "What does it mean to think like a mathematician?")

1. **Pre-rigorous** (K-14): Based on examples, intuition, and computation
2. **Rigorous** (upper division undergrad to graduate): Precise, formal; emphasizes theory
3. **Post-rigorous** (late graduate and beyond): Revisit and refine pre-rigorous intuition, buttressed by rigorous theory

## Key Insight: Rigour Should Serve Intuition

"The point of rigour is not to destroy all intuition; instead, it should be used to destroy bad intuition while clarifying and elevating good intuition."

"The ideal state to reach is when every heuristic argument naturally suggests its rigorous counterpart, and vice versa."

## The Freedom to Fail

"Because of this, the natural problem solving strategy in mathematics is to keep trying all sorts of approaches, even if they are likely to fail, but then draw lessons from their failure to inform future attempts."

"I once worked on a difficult problem in partial differential equations with five coauthors, including the legendary Jean Bourgain... After a few months, it seemed that the approach we wanted to try worked! We planned celebrations... But one of my coauthors noticed that we had missed an error term... It was only after two years of many different attempts that we finally solved the problem. It is still one of the papers I am the most proud of in my career."

## Asking "Dumb" Questions

"An expert is a person who has made all the mistakes that can be made in a very narrow field." - Niels Bohr

"Don't just read it; fight it! Ask your own questions, look for your own examples, discover your own proofs." - Paul Halmos

## Key Problem-Solving Advice

1. **Playing with problems** - even after solving them - is very helpful
2. **Long-term goal** is understanding, not just solving
3. **Partial progress** should be valued as a stepping stone
4. **If you can't explain it**, you haven't really understood it
5. **Write down what you've done** - don't rely on memory
6. **Continually aim just beyond your current range** - not too easy, not too hard
7. **Replace hard problems with simpler versions** - special cases, toy models
8. **Collaborate with people in adjacent fields**

## "Ask Yourself Dumb Questions"

This is one of Tao's most important heuristics. Example: When a student had three versions of Taylor approximation and didn't know which to use, the solution was to just try one, see if it works, fail, adjust.

## Implications for IntuitMath

- The three-phase model (pre-rigorous -> rigorous -> post-rigorous) should be embedded in our skill's DNA
- We should always present the "pre-rigorous" (intuitive) version FIRST
- The "freedom to fail" philosophy should be explicitly stated
- The emphasis on partial progress aligns with our "bold attempts" paradigm
- The idea that heuristics and rigor should inform each other (not compete)
